Research on Sim-to-Real in Reinforcement Learning (Apr. 2019 - Jan. 2022)
Reinforcement learning is advancing in applications such as robot control, but training robots to interact in the real world can be costly and risky. A solution is to operate agents in the real world that were pre-trained in simulators, but accurate simulators are not always available. Especially for vision-based agents that receive camera images as state observations, it is generally difficult to equip simulators with high-precision renderers. The problem of bridging the gap between simulators and the real world in reinforcement learning is studied in the field known as Sim-to-Real. We propose a method for effectively utilizing agents pre-trained in simulators in the real world, even under challenging conditions with only low-precision renderers, or sometimes even without any renderers at all. The paper on this study was accepted at the IJCNN, with an acceptance rate of 55.4% (~ 1467/2646 for IEEE WCCI).

Research on Neural Architecture SearchApr. 2018 - 2022 Neural Architecture Search (NAS) algorithmically optimizes the architecture of deep neural networks, as the structure of a computational graph significantly impacts the performance of a trained model. One of the main approaches in NAS is gradient-based NAS, which defines differentiable architectures to simultaneously optimize both the architecture and weight parameters via gradient descent. We focused on the time and memory requirements of gradient-based NAS, proposing a method that converges with fewer updates while minimizing GPU memory usage. This research, which I conducted as the first author, received JSAI Annual Conference Student Incentive Award in Jul. 2019 and was accepted at AAAI with an acceptance rate of 21.4% (~1692/7911). Additionally, I contributed to research on automatic termination for NAS, which exploits an upper bound on the decay of generalization error to determine when to stop the search process.
